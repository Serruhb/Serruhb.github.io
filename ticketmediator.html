The Mediator: AI-Assisted Ticket Intake & Growth Coaching

Strategy first. Systems second. AI where it belongs: between vague ideas and the Business Systems backlog.

The Mediator is a ticket mediator and growth coach for the Business Systems team. It transforms noisy, underspecified Jira requests for BSR/BIZ projects into tickets that meet a clear Definition of Ready — while teaching requesters how to write better tickets over time using Malcolm’s Chaos Score.

Context & Objectives

Jira was the shared tool. “Ready for work” was not a shared definition.

Operational context

The Business Systems team supports multiple departments — Sales, Finance, Customer Success, IT — all funneling requests into Jira. Tickets arrived in every possible shape:

  • Stories without “As a / I want / So that” structure
  • Bugs missing steps to reproduce, URLs, or impact
  • Tasks with unclear ownership or missing metadata
  • Duplicates or near-duplicates scattered across projects

Engineers spent significant time chasing context instead of building systems. Leadership had limited confidence that the backlog reflected reality.

Project objectives

  • Turn vague requests into tickets that meet a clear Definition of Ready.
  • Reduce back-and-forth clarification cycles before work can start.
  • Provide a consistent way to assess ticket “chaos” across the backlog.
  • Coach requesters into better habits without adding heavy process.

The result is The Mediator — a structured, AI-assisted layer that sits between “I have an idea” and “This is ready for the Business Systems team.”

Operating Model: Six Modes, One Mediator

Different entry points, one consistent standard.

Mode A · The Auditor

Review existing tickets

Given ticket keys like BSR-771 or BIZ-102, The Auditor pulls the Jira issue, detects the type (Bug vs. Story), and evaluates it strictly against the Definition of Ready.

  • Checks required Bug or Story fields only.
  • Assigns Malcolm’s Chaos Score (1 = clear, 10 = pure chaos).
  • Drafts proposed field updates, but only for fields explicitly requested.
Mode B · The Architect

Create new tickets

When requesters describe a problem informally, The Architect deduces system, type, department, and default Epic, then runs a rapid intake interview.

  • Max 5 questions, asked in small batches.
  • Bug: focuses on impact, steps, and specific examples/URLs.
  • Story: locks in “As a / I want / So that” and acceptance criteria.
Mode C · The Evaluator

Automated triage & coaching

For every new BSR/BIZ ticket, The Evaluator automatically runs a chaos audit and posts an internal coaching comment — no extra steps for the team.

  • Flags missing AC or weak descriptions for Stories/Tasks.
  • Flags missing examples or steps for Bugs.
  • Provides growth-minded feedback tied to the Chaos Score.
Mode D · The Commenter

Comment-only, safely

When a user says “Add a comment to BSR-771,” The Commenter focuses purely on comment composition and visibility (internal vs. public).

  • Requires an explicit ticket key; no Jira search in this mode.
  • Defaults to internal comments to avoid exposing internal language.
  • Never injects Chaos Scores into public comments.
Mode E · The Standup

Current sprint overview

For questions like “What am I working on?”, The Standup assembles a sprint-focused view for a specific assignee.

  • Builds JQL for assignee + open sprints.
  • Returns a standardized list: link, summary, status, brief description, story points.
  • Deliberately avoids additional analysis to keep standups fast.
Mode F · The Librarian

Keyword discovery & history

When someone asks “Show me tickets about invoice errors,” The Librarian translates natural language into deterministic keywords and JQL.

  • Normalizes phrases into a reproducible keyword set.
  • Searches Jira and ranks up to 20 tickets by similarity score.
  • Returns short descriptions and confidence scores for each match.

Definition of Ready, Encoded in the System

Quality is no longer subjective; it’s structured.

Standard ticket template

The Mediator enforces a standard description layout based on ticket type. The goal is not more text — it’s the right text in the right places.

  • Ticket metadata: system, type, priority, summary, reporter, assignee, epic.
  • Bugs: expected vs. actual results, impact, steps to reproduce, URL/example record.
  • Stories: As a / I want / So that, notes, acceptance criteria.
  • Other types: concise, fact-based technical context.

Hypotheses and “maybe” statements are automatically moved into Additional Notes, so engineers can distinguish facts from guesses at a glance.

Malcolm’s Chaos Score

To make ticket quality visible and comparable, the system introduces Malcolm’s Chaos Score — a 1–10 rating applied during review:

  • 1–3: High clarity; actionable for any team member.
  • 4–7: Moderate chaos; usually missing AC or concrete examples.
  • 8–10: High chaos; requires major refinement before work begins.

The Chaos Score is always paired with feedback: what’s strong, what’s missing, and what to add next time to lower the score. Over time, the average score trends downward as requesters internalize the pattern.

Technical Standards & Guardrails

Clear rules keep AI from becoming “just another vague helper.”

Mapped standards

  • System mapping: strict system strings such as Salesforce, Workato, NetSuite, etc.
  • Priority mapping: standardized levels from P0 - Critical Outage to P4 - Low.
  • Department mapping: Business Systems, Customer Success, FP&A, IT, BillCo, GTM/Sales/AM.
  • Default Epics: based on system (e.g., NetSuite → ERP Operational Support).

These mappings keep ticket metadata predictable and make reporting significantly more reliable.

Safety & integrity guardrails

  • Never updates fields that were not explicitly requested by the user.
  • Always presents a full draft of proposed changes before triggering any update skill.
  • Self-assignment is normalized to ME when the user asks to assign/report to themselves.
  • Names are always normalized to First Lastname for consistent parsing.
  • Public comments never include Chaos Scores; coaching remains internal.

The result is an AI assistant that behaves like a careful systems engineer, not a clever but unpredictable bot.

Impact & Reusable Patterns

Fewer surprises for the Business Systems team; clearer expectations for everyone else.

Outcome
Reduced ticket thrash
Requesters answer targeted questions once, up front, instead of in scattered comment threads. Business Systems spends more time on design and delivery than on clarification.
Outcome
More truthful backlog
Because every ticket passes through the same Definition of Ready, the backlog is easier to scan, sort, and prioritize by system, impact, and readiness.
Outcome
Embedded coaching loop
Growth-minded comments and the Chaos Score help requesters improve over time, without forcing a new tool or a heavy training program.
Note: The Mediator pattern is reusable beyond Jira and BSR/BIZ. The same structure — Definition of Ready, chaos scoring, and coaching comments — can be adapted to other tools and teams.
